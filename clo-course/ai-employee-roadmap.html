The AI Employee Roadmap

A step-by-step path from your first AI Employee to a set of them that run your work for you. Each phase below tells you what it is, what to do, and gives you prompts to paste straight into Claude Code. Check steps off as you go — your progress is saved on this device.

New to this? You don't have to memorize anything. Open a phase, follow the steps, and when in doubt just tell Claude Code what's next — it reads where you are and walks you through the next move.

The path

Seven phases. The first two happen during onboarding — tick them off if you've done them. The rest are yours to work through, and they don't have to be in order: jump to whichever one matches what you're hitting now.

1✓ Map your business onboardingStart here — find the one job worth handing off first ▾

Before you build anything, you find the one job that's worth handing to AI first. The Business X-Ray asks you about how your business runs, then points at the spot that's slowing you down most. Build for that, not for whatever's top of mind.

Do this

  • In Claude Code, say map my business (onboarding runs this for you).
  • Answer its questions about how your business actually works — plain language is fine.
  • Read the result. It names your highest-leverage thing to automate. That's your first build.

Prompts to copy

Map my business and show me the one job I should hand to an AI Employee first.

Or just say: "map my business"

2✓ Build your first AI Employee onboardingTurn that one job into a skill that does it for you ▾

Now you build it. Most first builds are a skill — a task you've taught Claude to do the same way every time (draft a newsletter, write a proposal). Sometimes it's a digital asset (a template or database) or a connection to a tool you use. Don't start from a blank page — pick a starter prompt below that matches your work, paste it in, and answer the questions.

Do this

  • Pick the job from your X-Ray (or the one you're most tired of doing).
  • Copy a starter prompt below that's closest to your work and paste it into Claude Code.
  • Answer its questions — it writes the skill and tests it with you.
  • Try your new skill by saying its trigger phrase. You just built an AI Employee.

Starter prompts — pick your kind of work

Build me a skill that turns a YouTube transcript into a first-draft newsletter in my voice, with 3 subject-line options.

Build me a skill that repurposes one newsletter into a Twitter/X thread and a LinkedIn post, keeping my voice.

Build me a skill that takes a rough idea and returns 5 hooks, with the angle behind each one.

Build me a skill that turns my sales-call notes into a proposal with scope, timeline, and 3 pricing options.

Build me a skill that drafts a follow-up email sequence after a sales call, using the prospect's stated goals.

Build me a skill that preps me for a sales call from the prospect's website and our past emails.

Build me a skill that turns a signed client into an onboarding checklist, a welcome email, and an intake form.

Build me a skill that writes a weekly client status update from my project notes.

Build me a skill that turns a finished project into a case-study draft and a testimonial request.

Build me a skill that turns my raw numbers into a monthly business report with the 3 things that moved.

Build me a skill that triages my inbox into reply-now / delegate / archive, with draft replies.

Build me a skill that turns a messy meeting transcript into decisions, action items with owners, and open questions.

Or just say: "create a skill for [the job]"

3✓ Make it reliable — add a harnessYour skill works but sometimes drifts? Lock it in. ▾

A new skill usually works — until one day the output wanders, or it ignores one of its own rules. The fix isn't to nag it with more instructions. It's a harness: an automatic check that runs every time and catches the slip before it reaches you. You don't write it — you tell Claude what "good" looks like and it builds the check.

When you're ready

Your skill works, but its output sometimes drifts, or it keeps breaking a rule you gave it (a banned word, a format, a length).

Do this

  • Run your skill a few times and note exactly where it slips (the rule it breaks).
  • Tell Claude to harden it — name the slip so the check knows what to catch.
  • Run it again and confirm the check catches the slip before you do.

Prompts to copy

Harden my [skill name] so it can't drift. Add an automatic check that runs every time.

My [skill name] keeps [the mistake — e.g. using em-dashes / going off-brand]. Add a harness that catches that before it reaches me.

Or just say: "harden this skill"

4✓ Connect skills into a systemOne skill needs another? Chain them into one flow. ▾

A single job is often several steps: intake → do the work → check it → hand it off. When one skill needs another to run first, you chain them into a skill system — an AI Employee that does the whole job, not one slice of it.

When you're ready

You catch yourself running one skill, then copying its output into another. Those steps belong together.

Do this

  • Write down the steps of the bigger job, in the order they happen.
  • Tell Claude to build a skill system that runs those steps as one flow.
  • Run the whole chain end to end and watch it hand off cleanly between steps.

Prompts to copy

Build me a skill system for [the whole job]. The steps are: [step 1] then [step 2] then [step 3].

Chain my [skill A] and [skill B] into one flow so I don't have to move the work between them.

Or just say: "build a skill system for this"

5✓ Hand work to an agentWant it to run on its own and report back? ▾

Some jobs you don't want to walk through step by step — you want to say "go do this and report back." That's an agent: it runs on its own, in the background or alongside other work, and hands you the result. Use it when supervising each step is the slow part.

When you're ready

The work could run in the background or in parallel, and you'd rather get a finished report than babysit it.

Do this

  • Pick a job you'd rather delegate than watch (research, a first pass, a batch).
  • Tell Claude to create an agent for it — say what "done" looks like.
  • Let it run, then read its report and adjust if needed.

Prompts to copy

Create an agent that [does the job] on its own and reports back when it's done. Here's what "done" looks like: [your standard].

Make a background agent for [the task] so I can keep working while it runs.

Or just say: "create an agent for this"

6✓ Make a shortcut — a commandDoing the same thing a lot? Give it one word. ▾

When you run the same thing the same way over and over, stop re-explaining it. Turn it into a command — a /shortcut you type once and Claude runs the whole routine.

When you're ready

You've typed nearly the same request more than a few times. It wants a name.

Do this

  • Notice a request you make the same way again and again.
  • Tell Claude to make a command for it and pick a short name.
  • Run it by typing /yourcommand.

Prompts to copy

Make a command for [the routine] so I can run it by typing one short slash command.

Or just say: "make a command for this"

7✓ Put it on autopilot — hooks, loops, schedulesTake yourself out of the loop entirely. ▾

The last step is removing yourself. A hook fires automatically on an event (run a check after every save). A schedule runs the work on its own (every Monday at 9am). A loop repeats until something's done. Now the outcome happens whether or not you remember it.

When you're ready

Something should happen on an event, or on a clock, without you starting it.

Do this

  • Decide what should run without you — on an event, or on a schedule.
  • Tell Claude to set it up (a hook for events, a schedule for a clock).
  • Confirm it runs on its own at least once.

Prompts to copy

Create a hook that [does the thing] automatically every time [the event happens].

Run my [skill] on a schedule — [e.g. every Monday at 9am] — and send me the result.

Or just say: "create a hook for this" or "run this on a schedule"

↻  Then build the next one

When this AI Employee is reliable, connected, and automatic — map the next bottleneck and do it again. Growth here isn't a longer to-do list; it's more employees, each taken up this same ladder. Say "what's next" or "map my business" to start the next one.

"When do I…" — quick cheat sheet

A fast gut-check for which tool fits. (Claude makes these calls with you — this is just so the words make sense.)

…split a skill?
When it's trying to do two jobs and keeps forgetting one. Two jobs → two skills that hand off.
…add a harness?
When a skill works but drifts, or breaks its own rule. An automatic check beats more instructions.
…use an agent?
When the work should run in the background or in parallel — "go do this and report back."
…make a command?
When you do the same thing the same way, often, and want a one-word shortcut.
…put it on autopilot?
When it should run on a schedule or fire on an event, with no one starting it.
…build the next one?
When the current employee is solid on all of the above. Map the next bottleneck and repeat.
